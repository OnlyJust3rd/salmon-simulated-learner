High-school mathematics provides a broad foundation of quantitative and logical
reasoning that supports the study of computing, science, engineering, and data. It
is general knowledge that more advanced material can take for granted, and several
of its strands recur, often in disguised form, throughout technical work.
Understanding this baseline means knowing what mathematical reasoning a learner
can be expected to bring to a new subject.

A foundational strand is sets and logic. Students learn to group objects into sets
and to reason about their membership, unions, and intersections, and to handle
statements that are either true or false, combining them with the connectives
"and," "or," and "not." This logical reasoning is not an abstract curiosity: it
maps directly onto how databases filter and combine data, where rows are selected
according to conditions joined by exactly these connectives, and onto the
conditionals that control the flow of every computer program.

Growing directly out of sets is the idea of a relation. Given two sets, a relation
is simply a collection of ordered pairs that links a value from one set to a value
from the other — for instance, pairing each student with the subjects they take.
Because a relation is just a set of these linked records, the natural way to write
one down is as a table: each row is one pair, and the columns hold the values being
related, so the relation becomes a grid of rows and columns of values. Students
learn to read a relation as such a table, to tell rows apart, and to follow how one
value points to another. A function, met next, is then just a special, tidy
relation in which each input value appears in only one row and maps to exactly one
output.

Closely related is the central idea of a function — a rule that maps each input to
exactly one output. Functions are the backbone of nearly everything that follows
in both mathematics and programming, where the same word describes a reusable
piece of code that turns inputs into outputs. Students study several important
families of functions in detail. Linear and quadratic functions describe straight
lines and parabolas. Exponential and logarithmic functions model growth and decay
and act as inverses of one another, appearing throughout science, finance, and the
analysis of how quickly algorithms run. Trigonometry deals with angles and
triangles and with the wave-like sine, cosine, and tangent functions that describe
anything periodic, from sound to alternating current.

Students also examine sequences and series — ordered patterns of numbers and the
sums of their terms — which introduce the ideas of progression and accumulation.
These lead into the basics of calculus, perhaps the crowning topic of school
mathematics. Differentiation measures rates of change, the slope of a curve at a
point, answering questions about how fast a quantity is changing; integration
measures accumulation, the area under a curve, answering questions about totals
built up from many small pieces. Together they give a language for change and
accumulation that underlies physics, engineering, economics, and machine learning.

The curriculum further covers tools for handling structure and uncertainty.
Vectors represent quantities that have both a magnitude and a direction, in two- or
three-dimensional space, and are essential to physics and to computer graphics,
where positions, movements, and forces are all vectors. Matrices are rectangular
grids of numbers used to organize data and to solve systems of linear equations
efficiently; they are fundamental to computer graphics, to data processing, and to
the linear algebra that powers modern data science and artificial intelligence.
Probability and statistics introduce the mathematics of chance and of data: how
likely events are, how to summarize and describe data sets, and how data is
distributed. This strand underpins all of modern data analysis, experimentation,
and machine learning, and trains students to reason carefully about uncertainty
and evidence.

None of these topics is, on its own, specialized to computing; together they form
the ordinary mathematical literacy expected of a high-school graduate. Their value
as a baseline lies in how often later technical material quietly relies on them.
When a database course speaks of counting records, filtering by a condition,
sorting results, or averaging values, it is drawing on this foundation of
arithmetic, logic, ordering, and basic statistics. When a programming course
introduces functions, variables, and loops, it echoes the mathematical ideas of
functions, unknowns, and sequences. Recognizing this baseline means understanding
which mathematical reasoning a learner can be assumed to possess, so that new
technical concepts can be explained in terms they already command.
